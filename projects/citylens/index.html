Evaluating large vision-language models for urban socioeconomic sensing.

Tianhui Liu, Hetian Pang, Xin Zhang, Tianjian Ouyang, Zhiyuan Zhang, Jie Feng, Pan Hui, and Yong Li

CityLens asks whether LVLMs can move from seeing cities to estimating their socioeconomic conditions, using satellite and street view imagery across global urban regions.

Abstract

Urban AI needs benchmarks that measure more than scene recognition.

CityLens is a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating whether large vision-language models can predict socioeconomic indicators from satellite and street view imagery. It covers 17 globally distributed cities, 11 prediction tasks, and 6 domains: economy, education, crime, transport, health, and environment. The benchmark evaluates 17 state-of-the-art LVLMs under three paradigms, revealing both promising perceptual capabilities and persistent limitations in numerical grounding, cross-city generalization, and abstract socioeconomic inference.

Benchmark

A multi-modal, multi-city benchmark for socioeconomic sensing.

Each evaluation region is represented by one satellite image, ten street view images, and a scalar socioeconomic label. The benchmark spans global and country-specific tasks, with geographic units aligned to satellite areas, census tracts, or MSOAs.

Coverage 17 global cities

Including cities across North America, Europe, Asia, Africa, South America, and Oceania.

Signals 6 domains

Economy, education, crime, transport, health, and environment.

Tasks 11 indicators

GDP, population, house price, public transport, drive ratio, mental health, and more.

Models 17 LVLMs

Open and proprietary systems are evaluated under the same task definitions.

Evaluation Paradigms

Three ways to test what LVLMs can actually do.

CityLens separates direct numeric prediction, relative normalized estimation, and feature-based regression so that perception, ranking, and numeric grounding can be analyzed independently.

01

Direct Metric Prediction

The model receives urban imagery and directly outputs a specific socioeconomic value, such as the public transit ratio or average house price for a region.

02

Normalized Metric Estimation

The target is converted to a 0.0 to 9.9 scale, emphasizing relative ordering and reducing the burden of absolute value calibration.

03

Feature-Based Regression

LVLMs score structured visual features, then a regression model maps those features to socioeconomic indicators, turning the LVLM into a visual feature enhancer.

Findings

Current LVLMs see useful urban cues, but still struggle to quantify society.

CityLens reveals that visual grounding, model architecture, task design, and data coverage all matter. Larger models are not automatically better, and visually grounded indicators are far easier than latent social outcomes.

Observable tasks perform better.

Building Height, Public Transport, and GDP show stronger results because their signals are more visually tied to skyline structure, transit infrastructure, and urban morphology.

Abstract indicators remain hard.

Mental Health, Life Expectancy, and Bachelor Ratio depend on latent social and health mechanisms that are not cleanly visible in street or satellite imagery.

Street views are especially valuable.

Ground-level imagery provides dense semantic cues such as building facades, signage, road quality, and public infrastructure. More street views tend to improve performance.

Geographic gaps are measurable.

The GDP bias audit reports stronger performance for Global North cities (R2 0.399, nRMSE 0.787) than Global South cities (R2 0.168, nRMSE 0.869).

Takeaway

CityLens is not just a leaderboard. It is a diagnostic lens.

The benchmark makes it possible to ask sharper questions: Which indicators are visually observable? Which models extract stable urban concepts? When should LVLMs predict directly, estimate normalized scores, or provide structured visual features?

By exposing failure modes across cities, modalities, and socioeconomic domains, CityLens turns urban visual intelligence into a measurable research problem.
